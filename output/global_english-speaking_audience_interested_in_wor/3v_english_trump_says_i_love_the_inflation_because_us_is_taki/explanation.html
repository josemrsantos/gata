Explaining the Cartoon: "Inflation Is Victory"

This three-panel cartoon features Gata — the Gata Newsroom's signature tabby journalist — seated at a heavy metal desk in a dimly lit, 1970s-era newsroom. Behind her is a chalkboard. Over the course of the three panels, that chalkboard does all the talking. If the joke landed immediately for you, you likely have a working knowledge of George Orwell, Turkish economic policy, the 1973 oil crisis, and Venezuelan political theatre. If any of those references are unfamiliar, this page is for you.

What Actually Happened: The News Peg

On June 10, 2026, a sitting head of government made two statements in close proximity to each other. First, his administration announced it had seized oil — taking control of oil supplies or oil-related assets as a policy action. Second, the same leader publicly declared, in words reported directly, that he "loves the inflation." His government had previously attributed rising prices — inflation — at least in part to oil-related factors.

The cartoon's first panel captures the logical problem in chalk on a board: if seizing oil is your remedy for high prices, the arrow on your own diagram should point downward on the price scale. Instead, the board shows the arrow pointing toward "PRICES UP." The chalkboard is not editorialising. It is simply recording the direction of the arrow the speaker's own logic implies.

Panel One: The Setup — The Arrow That Points the Wrong Way

Gata stares at the board with what the cartoon describes as a "composed, tired expression." She is not angry. She is not amused. She has simply noticed that the arithmetic does not resolve. The board reads: "SEIZED OIL = LOWER PRICES?" The question mark is doing considerable work. Standard economic reasoning holds that restricting or disrupting oil supply tends to raise energy costs, which ripples outward into broader price increases across an economy. Seizing oil — removing it from normal market circulation — is not, in the consensus view, a standard tool for reducing inflation. Gata's tired expression is the expression of someone who has been asked to square a circle before.

Panel Two: The Escalation — Pattern Recognition

The second panel widens the lens. Gata turns slightly toward the viewer. The chalkboard now shows a timeline labelled "PATTERN RECOGNITION," connecting three historical data points before circling one word at the bottom: "PRECEDENT."

The three entries on the board are:

1973: Oil Withheld → Prices Up. In October 1973, the Organisation of Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC) — led by Arab member states — imposed an oil embargo on nations that had supported Israel during the Yom Kippur War. The United States, Western Europe, and Japan were primary targets. The result was a quadrupling of global oil prices and fuel shortages that defined the economic anxieties of an entire decade. The 1973 embargo is the foundational modern lesson that oil supply manipulation translates directly into consumer suffering. It is the reason a chalkboard arrow pointing from "OIL" to "PRICES UP" requires no further annotation for anyone with a basic knowledge of economic history.

Ankara: Rates Up → Inflation (allegedly). This entry refers to Turkish President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan's long-running and internationally scrutinised position that raising interest rates causes inflation, rather than suppressing it. Orthodox economics — the position held by virtually every central bank in the world — holds the opposite: raising interest rates cools an overheating economy by making borrowing more expensive, which reduces spending and thereby reduces upward pressure on prices. Erdoğan repeatedly pressured Turkey's central bank to cut rates even as inflation rose, and dismissed economists who disagreed. His position is not a fringe view held in ignorance; it is a deliberate policy choice to treat economic consensus as merely one opinion among many — one that happens to be politically inconvenient. Turkey experienced severe inflation in the years that followed. Erdoğan's example established a modern template: a leader who does not deny that inflation exists, but insists that everyone else has the cause-and-effect relationship backwards.

Caracas: Scarcity = Sovereignty. This entry refers to Venezuela under President Nicolás Maduro. Venezuela, despite holding the world's largest proven oil reserves, has experienced some of the most severe hyperinflation of any country in the 21st century. At its peak, Venezuela's annual inflation rate was measured in the millions of percent. Through those years, Maduro's government routinely announced economic victories, blamed external enemies — particularly the United States — for shortages, and framed scarcity not as a failure of governance but as evidence that Venezuela was resisting imperial pressure. The performance of economic triumph amid measurable economic collapse is now, for globally literate audiences, immediately recognisable as a genre — a set of rhetorical moves that travel across borders and governments.

All three points connect on the chalkboard to one circled word: "PRECEDENT." Gata's tail is still. She does not look surprised. The cartoon's point is not that this is a new phenomenon. It is that the phenomenon has a documented history, a recognisable grammar, and a clear lineage.

Panel Three: The Punchline — The Ministry Slogan

In the final panel, Gata rises slightly and looks directly and flatly at the viewer. The chalkboard now bears a single large inscription, centred, in the visual style of an official wall slogan:

INFLATION IS VICTORY
(cf. War is Peace, 1949)

The footnote — "(cf. War is Peace, 1949)" — is the cartoon's most compressed and most devastating reference. "War is Peace" is one of three Party slogans from George Orwell's novel Nineteen Eighty-Four, published in 1949. The three slogans of the Party in Orwell's fictional totalitarian state of Oceania are:

WAR IS PEACE
FREEDOM IS SLAVERY
IGNORANCE IS STRENGTH

Each slogan works by taking a concept that is universally understood to be harmful or undesirable — war, slavery, ignorance — and declaring it to be identical with its opposite. The purpose, in Orwell's analysis, is not to deceive the population about facts they cannot verify. The purpose is to demonstrate power: to show that the Party controls not just behaviour but meaning itself. If the Party says war is peace, then peace is what war is, and to say otherwise is to commit thoughtcrime. The concept Orwell gave this rhetorical manoeuvre is "doublespeak" — the use of language not to communicate truth but to replace it.

"Inflation Is Victory" follows the same grammatical structure. Inflation is a condition that measurably reduces the purchasing power of ordinary people's wages and savings. It is not a matter of political interpretation that prices rising faster than incomes makes people poorer. To announce that you "love the inflation" — and to frame a policy action that economic logic suggests may worsen it as a remedy — is not a factual error. It is an attempt to reassign meaning: to take the thing the audience correctly identifies as harmful and declare it, by rhetorical fiat, to be a trophy.

Gata's expression in the final panel is "perfectly neutral — no raised brow, no smirk, no emotion." This is the cartoon's formal joke. The satirist's usual tools are exaggeration, mockery, or outrage. Gata deploys none of them. She simply holds up the slogan next to its 1949 ancestor and lets the family resemblance speak. The caption completes the thought: "The slogan required no focus group. It had already been road-tested."

Why It Is Funny — and Why the Humour Is Also Grim

The cartoon operates in a specific comedic register that is sometimes called "deadpan absurdism." The joke is not that the situation is ridiculous — though it is. The joke is that Gata refuses to perform outrage or surprise, because neither is warranted. Outrage would imply the behaviour is unexpected. Surprise would imply the pattern is new. The chalkboard — the cartoon's real protagonist — methodically demonstrates that neither is true. The humour comes from the gap between the gravity of what is being described and the clinical composure with which Gata describes it.

There is a secondary joke embedded in the cartoon's visual grammar. A chalkboard in a newsroom is deliberately old-fashioned — the kind of tool a teacher might use to walk students through a logical proof. By presenting the analysis this way, the cartoon implies that what is being explained should be elementary. The arrow points up. The historical precedents are documented. The slogan has a known author. None of this requires sophisticated analysis. The joke is that it keeps needing to be explained anyway.

The grimness underneath the humour is that Orwell's novel was intended as a warning, and the warning has not prevented the thing it warned against. Each new iteration of the "War is Peace" structure — each new government that declares a measurable harm to be a public good — arrives already equipped with a rhetorical infrastructure that Orwell mapped in 1949. The cartoon's final footnote, "(cf. War is Peace, 1949)," is a citation in the academic sense: it is pointing you to the primary source so you can verify that this pattern has been described before, in detail, by someone who understood it very well.

In Summary

The cartoon shows Gata, a composed tabby journalist, watching a chalkboard accumulate evidence that a leader's declaration of love for inflation — paired with an oil seizure — belongs to a well-documented tradition of political semantic inversion: taking a public harm, renaming it a trophy, and daring the audience to disagree. The tradition runs from the 1973 oil embargo through Erdoğan's interest-rate heterodoxy and Maduro's scarcity-as-sovereignty performances, and its intellectual framework was most precisely described by George Orwell in 1949. The cartoon's final panel places the latest entry in that tradition directly beneath its source text. Gata offers no further comment. None is required.
