**REVIEW 1 — First instinct too obvious or too lenient?**
The obvious joke is "Chega chegou ao poder, ironia do nome." That's the surface read every pundit already made. Sharper: the uncomfortable truth isn't that Chega won — it's that the conditions for Chega were *built* by the people now most horrified by it. The real joke is the mirror, not the monster in it. The strip should implicate the audience, not just the villain.

**REVIEW 3 — Material improvement still missing?**
The "Chega" double meaning (the slogan against corruption *becoming* the arrival of something worse) is the richest vein. But the deepest cut is the **25 de Abril** angle: Portugal spent 52 years using the Carnation Revolution as moral armour — and the ultimate punchline is that the cravo vermelho didn't protect anyone because it became a *decorative* object, worn by the same class that made Chega inevitable. The three panels should trace this: (1) the cravo as shield, (2) the cravo as decorative object on the desks of the NHR elite, (3) the cravo wilted in a government anteroom — the revolution as wallpaper in the new administration's waiting room. This is the strip that makes the audience squirm, because it doesn't let anyone off the hook.
