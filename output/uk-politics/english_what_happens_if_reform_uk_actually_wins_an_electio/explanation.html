Cartoon Explained

What Happens If Reform UK Actually Wins? — The Joke, Unpacked

The cartoon published by Gata Newsroom shows our correspondent Gata — a calico-tabby cat of grave professional demeanour — sitting before a large chalkboard in a 1970s fluorescent-lit newsroom. On the board is a hand-drawn flow chart under the heading ON THE SPOT. The chart traces what would logically happen if Reform UK won a general election. It ends at a box reading SEE ALSO: UKIP, 2016. Taped to the corner of the board is a scrap of paper reading: "Full costings available. Ask at bar." The caption beneath the panel reads:

"Winning, it turns out, was not part of the business model."

If you are puzzled by any of this, you are in the right place. Here is everything you need.

Who Are Reform UK?

Reform UK is a British right-wing populist party led by Nigel Farage. Its political identity is built almost entirely on opposition: opposition to immigration, to the political establishment, to what it characterises as an out-of-touch elite, and — in its earlier incarnation — to British membership of the European Union. The party and its predecessors have been extraordinarily effective at channelling public frustration. What they have been considerably less effective at is the business of actually governing anything.

This is not an accident or an oversight. It is, the cartoon argues, the entire point. A party whose brand is indignation requires, as its raw material, things to be indignant about. The moment such a party wins power and becomes responsible for the bins, the hospitals, the ferries, and the border — it can no longer be indignant about them. It is them. The protest evaporates. What remains is just a government, and governments are expected to govern.

The Flow Chart and Why It Is Structured as It Is

The chalkboard flow chart is designed to look like a piece of investigative journalism — a logical chain of cause and effect. Each step follows inevitably from the last, and the comedy builds precisely because the steps are so mundane. "Government must do things." "Things turn out to be hard." These are not extraordinary revelations; they are statements of the blindingly obvious. The joke is that for a protest party, the obvious is catastrophic.

The pivot point — and the heart of the satire — is the box reading [LONG PAUSE]. This single interjection does a great deal of work. It is the moment when the triumphant election night gives way to the Monday morning of having to actually run the country. The pause is not comic blankness; it is the specific, horrible silence of people who have spent years confidently explaining what everyone else is doing wrong, suddenly realising they are now the ones doing it.

The sequence "Who is in cabinet? / People who were very cross on TV / Still very cross. On TV." is a reference to the observable reality that Reform's most prominent figures have built their profiles through media appearances rather than ministerial experience. Being cross and articulate on a news panel is a genuine and marketable skill. It is not, however, the same skill as managing a government department, negotiating international trade agreements, or explaining to the Treasury why the numbers do not add up.

The UKIP Reference: A Cautionary Precedent

The final node of the flow chart — SEE ALSO: UKIP, 2016 — points to the most directly relevant historical example. The UK Independence Party spent the better part of two decades campaigning for Britain to leave the European Union. In June 2016, the Brexit referendum delivered precisely the result UKIP had demanded. Britain voted to leave.

UKIP then proceeded to implode almost immediately. Having achieved their singular purpose, the party had no unifying cause to hold it together. They cycled through leaders at a dizzying rate — Paul Nuttall, Henry Bolton, Gerard Batten, and others — while the party's membership and poll ratings collapsed. By the 2019 general election, UKIP was essentially finished as a serious political force. They had won the argument. They simply had no idea what to do after winning it.

The cartoon draws an explicit parallel: Reform, like UKIP before it, is a party optimised for the campaign rather than the consequence. Winning power would be, in a very real sense, the worst thing that could happen to it.

The Fag Packet Note: "Full Costings Available. Ask at Bar."

The small scrap of paper taped to the corner of the chalkboard is what satirists call a "reward for close readers" — a detail that sharpens the joke if you notice it, but does not interrupt the main flow if you do not.

The phrase "costed on the back of a fag packet" is a longstanding British idiom for financial planning that is hasty, rough, and wildly inadequate for the purpose. A fag packet is a cigarette packet — small, disposable, not generally associated with rigorous economic modelling. The phrase is used when critics want to suggest that a party's spending promises have not been seriously worked through. Reform's manifesto has attracted this criticism in various election cycles, with independent analysts questioning whether their pledges add up.

The instruction to "ask at bar" implies that the serious policy detail — the part that is meant to reassure you that all of this is affordable — is not on the official document at all. It exists only in informal conversation, delivered with confidence in a pub, by someone who has absolutely not done the sums.

The Nigel Farage Dimension

Nigel Farage, the figure most associated with Reform and its predecessor parties, stood for Parliament on seven separate occasions before finally winning a seat in Clacton at the 2024 general election. For most politicians, repeated failure to win a seat would represent career-ending embarrassment. For Farage, it functioned differently: he remained one of the most recognisable and influential figures in British politics throughout, largely through television, radio, and the sheer force of his media presence.

The cartoon's observation that Reform's people are "very cross on TV" and remain so even after a hypothetical election victory is a gentle glance at this dynamic. Farage is, by any measure, an exceptionally effective media performer. The open question — the one the cartoon poses — is whether the skills that make someone compelling on a panel show translate into the grinding, unglamorous, technically complex work of running a country.

The Thick of It: British Government as Chaos Engine

The Thick of It, Armando Iannucci's celebrated BBC political satire, depicted British government not as a purposeful machine but as a perpetual crisis managed by terrified, underprepared people whose primary skill was avoiding blame. The show became a definitive cultural reference for the gap between the public performance of political competence and the private reality of institutional dysfunction. When the cartoon imagines a Reform cabinet — assembled from people whose main qualification is being cross on television — it is implicitly invoking this tradition. The joke is that at least the governments satirised in The Thick of It had the decency to pretend they knew what they were doing. Reform, the cartoon implies, might not even manage that.

The SNP and the Difficulty of Actually Governing

The Scottish National Party provides a complementary reference point. The SNP has governed Scotland through the devolved Holyrood parliament since 2007, and their experience has illustrated a truth that applies to all parties but stings most acutely for those defined by a single transformative promise: governing is hard, and the day-to-day grind of ferries that do not float, procurement disasters, and budget shortfalls is not easily reconciled with the narrative of a movement on the cusp of national liberation. The SNP's record in government has, for many observers, demonstrated the distance between campaigning on a vision and delivering it in practice.

Change UK: The Fastest Route from Launch to Oblivion

Change UK — also briefly known as The Independent Group for Change, also known as the party whose logo remained a subject of genuine public bafflement — serves as a supporting footnote in the cartoon's broader argument. In early 2019, a group of MPs defected from Labour and the Conservatives to form a new centrist party amid considerable media fanfare. They were described as the future of British politics. Within months, they had failed to win a single seat in the European Parliament elections, suffered internal splits, and effectively ceased to exist as a meaningful political entity. They had, at one point, failed to secure their own domain name before their launch announcement. The speed of the collapse was almost admirable in its completeness.

Change UK is not directly referenced in the cartoon, but it inhabits the same satirical universe: the party that presented itself as an answer to a question, without having seriously considered what the question actually was.

Why the Caption Lands

The final caption — "Winning, it turns out, was not part of the business model." — works because it reframes the entire cartoon as a business analysis rather than a political attack. "Business model" is the language of rational planning, of entrepreneurship, of people who have thought carefully about sustainability and revenue and purpose. Applying it to a political party sounds almost generous, almost managerial. And then it delivers its verdict: the business model, analysed dispassionately, does not include winning. Winning is the one outcome the model cannot survive.

The phrase "it turns out" does additional quiet work. It implies discovery — as though this were a finding that surprised everyone, including perhaps Reform themselves. Of course it was not a surprise to anyone paying attention. But the polite fiction of surprise is itself part of the joke: the coroner's tone, the investigative gravity, the chalkboard flow chart — all of it treating as a serious inquiry something whose conclusion was obvious from the start.

Gata: The Reporter Who Has Seen It All Before

Gata, the Newsroom's resident correspondent, is rendered in full colour against a greyscale background — a visual technique that marks her as the only figure in the room who is fully present, fully awake, and fully unimpressed. Her expression is described as "grave and professionally exhausted." This is not the face of someone who finds the situation amusing. It is the face of someone who has filed this particular story before, under different names, and is filing it again with the resigned precision of a journalist who knows that the punchline never changes, only the cast.
