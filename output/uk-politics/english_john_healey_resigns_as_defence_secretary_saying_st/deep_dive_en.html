The Flag Was the Strategy: John Healey's Resignation and the Deflation of New New Labour

A single Union Jack on a chalkboard, a weary cat, and a receipt from the outgoing Defence Secretary. Here is everything you need to understand why this cartoon lands the way it does — and why British audiences will wince in recognition.

"After three years of proving Labour had changed on defence, the Defence Secretary confirmed it hadn't."

The News Background

When Keir Starmer became leader of the Labour Party in 2020, he inherited a party widely perceived as unserious on national security. His predecessor, Jeremy Corbyn, had a long record of opposing Western military interventions, expressing sympathy for groups that the British establishment regarded as hostile, and equivocating on questions like Trident — the UK's nuclear deterrent. The 2019 general election had been a catastrophe for Labour. Starmer's mission, from the very first week, was to reverse that damage.

He did so with an almost theatrical determination. Pledge cards. Union Jack backdrops at press conferences. Visits to military bases. Consistent vocal support for NATO, for Ukraine, for the armed forces. Members of the party who had criticised defence spending or expressed anti-war views found themselves disciplined or removed. By the time Labour won the 2024 general election and entered government, Starmer had, on paper, completed the transformation. Labour was credible on defence. The branding said so.

Then, in 2025, John Healey — the Defence Secretary whom Starmer had appointed to lead this newly serious defence agenda — resigned. His stated reason was, in essence, that the defence budget remained structurally inadequate for the threats the United Kingdom actually faces. After all the flags, all the photoshoots, all the expulsions and rebranding, the man responsible for British military capability concluded that British military capability was not properly funded. The gap between the presentation and the reality had been made official, by an insider, from inside the Cabinet itself.

What the Cartoon Shows — and Why

The image is deliberately, almost aggressively simple. On a large chalkboard headed On The Spot, there is a single carefully drawn Union Jack — the flag of the United Kingdom — labelled, with perfect deadpan, Defence Strategy (2021–2026). Nothing else. No spending figures, no plans, no structures. Just the flag. The implication is exact: the flag was the strategy. The signifier was the policy. The aesthetic of seriousness was substituted for seriousness itself.

Taped to the corner of the chalkboard is a small note: Receipt Enclosed — J. Healey. A receipt, in this context, is the bill for goods received — or, more precisely, a formal acknowledgement of a transaction. Healey's resignation letter is being read as the receipt: here is what you actually got for your money, here is the itemised account of what five years of flag-waving purchased. It is a document of accounting, and the account does not balance.

The cat — Gata, the Newsroom's editorial mascot — sits at a typewriter desk staring at the chalkboard with the expression of someone who has not been surprised. That flat, exhausted look is not ignorance; it is the recognition of someone who saw the gap between the performance and the policy back in 2021, when the pledge cards were still warm from the printer.

The Cultural References and Why They Resonate

The Union Jack as political prop is not accidental shorthand here — it is the specific prop Starmer chose, repeatedly and consciously, as his rebranding instrument. His 2021 pledge card, distributed to Labour members, was photographed against a large Union Jack in a move that commentators noted was almost comically emphatic. For British audiences, the flag in this cartoon is not decorative. It is a direct quotation of those images, now recontextualised as a chalkboard diagram of a strategy that turned out to consist mainly of the flag itself.

The ghost of the 1983 general election hovers over every Labour defence row. That year, under Michael Foot, Labour fought on a manifesto that included unilateral nuclear disarmament. The party lost catastrophically, and the manifesto was later memorably described by Gerald Kaufman — one of Labour's own MPs — as "the longest suicide note in history." Since then, no Labour leader can be seen to be soft on defence without triggering this association. Starmer's entire strategy was to exorcise that ghost. The uncomfortable irony exposed by Healey's resignation is that the exorcism was cosmetic: the ghost of inadequate defence policy is still rattling around in the actual budget, even if it has been fitted with a Union Jack.

Geoffrey Howe's 1990 resignation speech from Margaret Thatcher's Cabinet is the canonical British example of a polite letter that functions as a political detonation. Howe famously described the experience of serving under Thatcher as being sent to bat for England with a broken bat — the instructions were grand, the equipment was unusable. Healey's resignation borrows from this tradition of restraint-as-lethality. The letter is not theatrical. It is factual. And factual, in this context, is the most devastating register available.

The "defence black hole" — the chronic gap between announced defence commitments and actual funding — is a recurring feature of British political life that predates this government by decades. Voters and commentators have watched successive administrations, across all parties, discover the black hole with apparent astonishment, announce reviews, and then rediscover the same black hole four years later. Healey's resignation fits this tradition, but with the particular twist that it comes from inside a government that had made closing the credibility gap its founding purpose.

Trident — the submarine-based nuclear weapons system — has long served as a kind of political sacrament for Labour leaders seeking to prove their seriousness to a sceptical electorate. Endorsing Trident renewal is the specific gesture through which Labour leaders signal that they understand power and are willing to exercise it. The quiet irony woven through this cartoon is that Starmer performed the Trident ritual perfectly, while the conventional — non-nuclear — defence budget, which actually determines the day-to-day capacity of the armed forces, was left in the condition Healey's resignation describes. The congregation was given the liturgy; the roof was still leaking.

Why the Satirical Angle Works

The most effective political satire does not invent absurdity — it identifies the absurdity that is already structurally present in events and makes it visible in a single image. This cartoon's power comes from the precision of the equivalence it draws: Union Jack equals defence strategy. That equation is not an exaggeration. It is, arguably, a literal description of what the branding-first approach produced. The cartoon makes no claim that Healey is wrong, or that the situation is irredeemable, or that some other party would have done better. It simply presents the ledger.

The receipt metaphor carries particular weight because it is commercial, not political. It reframes a Cabinet resignation not as an ideological rupture or a personal betrayal, but as a consumer complaint — here is what you said you were selling, here is what was actually in the box. This is a deeply British mode of understatement. The polite, factual letter of disappointment, the itemised invoice, the quiet this is not what was advertised: these are the forms British culture uses to deliver its most corrosive verdicts.

The cat's exhaustion is the final piece. British satirical tradition has always had a place for the observer who was not deceived — the figure who registered the gap between claim and reality at the time, and who now watches the reckoning arrive without vindication, because vindication requires that things have been fixed, and they have not been. Gata does not gloat. She has simply been keeping the minutes.

The Central Irony, Stated Plainly

Keir Starmer spent the first three years of his leadership methodically, sometimes ruthlessly, remaking Labour's image on defence. He did so because he understood — correctly — that image had destroyed Labour before. What the Healey resignation reveals is that in solving the image problem, the policy problem was not similarly resolved. The flag was hoisted. The chequebook, as the cartoon's framing suggests, remained on the desk, unsigned. A receipt is only issued after a transaction is complete. Healey's letter is the receipt for a transaction that, it turns out, was never fully made.
