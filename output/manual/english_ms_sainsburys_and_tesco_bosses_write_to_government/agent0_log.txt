CULTURAL ANGLE: Three of Britain's most aggressive adopters of self-checkout machines, warehouse robotics, and algorithmic demand forecasting have written a *letter* to the government expressing grave concern about youth unemployment — which is, in the industry, known as "raising a ticket with someone else's team." The CEOs of M&S, Sainsbury's, and Tesco have essentially filed a GitHub issue labelled "youth unemployment: not our repo," having spent the last decade merging PRs titled "replace aisle staff with app," "automate fulfilment centre," and "Clubcard ML pricing optimisation." For British developers — many of whom have, at some point, built exactly this kind of labour-displacing system under a perfectly reasonable-sounding Jira ticket — there is something exquisitely familiar about watching the people who commissioned the automation now authoring a strongly worded letter to the government as their primary mitigation strategy. It is, in effect, a letter of redundancy about redundancy.

REFERENCES:
- The British "strongly worded letter" is a cherished national ritual — the preferred response to any crisis one wishes to acknowledge without actually doing anything about; Paddington Bear's hard stare made physical.
- Tesco's Clubcard has been quietly harvesting behavioural data on the British public since 1995; the idea that Tesco's boardroom is *surprised* by economic trends affecting its own customers requires a quite heroic suspension of disbelief.
- M&S spent years as the cautionary tale of a retailer that refused to modernise, then course-corrected so aggressively with tech and automation that it became the cautionary tale in the other direction — a classic big rewrite that nuked the legacy system along with the staff.
- Amazon's fulfilment centre expansion across the UK, widely covered in the British tech press, effectively set the automation benchmark that every supermarket logistics team was then asked to match — meaning many of the engineers who built those systems did so under competitive pressure from a single American dependency.
- The "not my problem, raise it with the government" instinct will be immediately recognisable to any developer who has ever watched a senior stakeholder escalate a bug caused by their own requirements as though it were an external incident.
