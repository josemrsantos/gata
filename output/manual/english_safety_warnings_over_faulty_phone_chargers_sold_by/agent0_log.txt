<verdict>
CULTURAL ANGLE: The nation's software engineers — professionals who spend their working hours writing defensive code, obsessing over null checks, and raising Jira tickets about theoretical edge cases that will never occur in production — have apparently been plugging unvalidated, uncertified chargers directly into the mains and going to bed. These are people who would never merge a PR without a code review, yet they have been cheerfully running hardware with no error handling, no safety certification, and an unknown manufacturer name that appears to be nine consonants arranged by a cat walking across a keyboard. The chargers, sourced from major UK retailers who presumably also passed some form of quality gate, represent what engineers would technically classify as an *unhandled exception in the physical layer* — one whose catch block is, regrettably, a kitchen fire at 3am. The deepest irony is structural: the same engineer who suppresses compiler warnings before a Friday deploy, mutters "it'll be fine in prod," and closes fourteen browser tabs of Stack Overflow answers they didn't fully read, is now being asked to take seriously a *safety* warning. At least this one doesn't require a restart to apply.

REFERENCES:
- The BS 1363 plug standard — Britain's famously robust, rectangular three-pin plug — is a quiet point of national engineering pride, regularly cited as one of the safest domestic plug designs in the world, which makes buying a charger that violates it feel like a particularly personal betrayal.
- The Samsung Galaxy Note 7's 2016 recall, in which the device was banned from flights for spontaneously combusting, is the canonical reference point for consumer electronics going thermally rogue — and yet somehow did not cause the nation's developers to start reading the small print on charger packaging.
- Poundland's electrical aisle has long occupied a specific cultural position in the British psyche: everyone knows, on some level, that a £1.99 USB charger is not subject to the same QA pipeline as a medical device, and yet the value proposition remains emotionally compelling at the point of purchase.
- The UK's Trading Standards office issues product recalls with the quiet, apologetic energy of a government department that has long since made peace with not being listened to — a tone software engineers will recognise from reading internal security advisories.
- "Works on my machine" is the engineering industry's most durable defence against quality concerns, and there is a pleasing structural parallel in the charger owner who notes that it has, in fact, charged seventeen phones without incident, and considers this a statistically significant sample size.
