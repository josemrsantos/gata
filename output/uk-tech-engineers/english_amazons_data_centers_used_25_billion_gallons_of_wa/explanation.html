Gata Newsroom — Cartoon Explainer

You Didn't Eliminate the Server. You Outsourced the Guilt, at Scale, with 99.99% Uptime.

This cartoon requires a little unpacking — not because the joke is obscure, but because it operates at several layers simultaneously, like a particularly well-structured piece of software that nonetheless turns out to be draining a river. What follows is a plain-English guide to why a calico-tabby cat sitting in front of a chalkboard covered in AWS architecture diagrams is, in fact, extremely funny — and more than a little damning.

What actually happened?

Amazon Web Services (AWS) — the cloud computing division of Amazon, and the infrastructure underpinning a substantial portion of the modern internet — disclosed that its data centres consumed approximately 2.5 billion gallons of water in the course of a single year. This water is used primarily for cooling: the servers that power everything from your streaming service to your company's production database generate enormous amounts of heat, and evaporating water is one of the most efficient ways to remove it. Amazon, for its part, noted that when measured against the amount of computing work performed, its water usage was more efficient than that of Microsoft or Google. This is the defence the cartoon is interrogating.

What is "the cloud," really?

For the past fifteen years or so, the technology industry has marketed "the cloud" as something inherently clean, weightless, and modern — a shift away from physical infrastructure towards something almost conceptual. Moving your application "to the cloud" was presented as a kind of dematerialisation. The servers were still there, of course — they were simply somewhere else, in vast warehouse-sized facilities, usually in places with cheap electricity and, it now turns out, significant access to water. The cartoon's central joke is the collision between that ethereal brand image and the very literal, very material reality: the cloud is, in the most basic meteorological sense, made of evaporated water. It always was.

What is "serverless," and why is that funny?

AWS Lambda is a product that allows developers to run code without managing a server directly. You provide the function; Amazon handles everything else. This model was marketed under the banner of "serverless computing" — one of the great feats of technology branding. It does not mean there are no servers. It means you personally do not have to think about the servers, which is not quite the same thing. The architecture diagram on the chalkboard — showing the neat, professional chain of boxes labelled "YOUR APP," "LAMBDA FUNCTION," "S3 BUCKET," "API GATEWAY," and "EC2 INSTANCE (us-east-1)" — is recognisable to any developer who has ever presented a system design at a team meeting or a tech conference. The arrow at the bottom, leading to a dripping tap and the words "2,500,000,000 GALLONS," completes the diagram. It is the missing box. It was always there. It just wasn't on the slide.

The hosepipe ban — a distinctly British institution

Each summer in Britain, when rainfall has been insufficient, water companies issue what is known as a hosepipe ban. Householders are formally prohibited from using garden hosepipes — no watering the lawn, no filling the paddling pool, no rinsing the car. This is treated with great seriousness. The contrast the cartoon draws is between the suburban homeowner forbidden from watering their hanging baskets under threat of a fine, and data centres of industrial scale continuing to consume water by the billion gallon, unimpeded and largely unnoticed. The small chalk sketch in the corner of the chalkboard — a hosepipe coiled beside a wheelie bin, crossed out, labelled "HOSEPIPE BAN: JUNE 2026" — makes this contrast explicit. The individual is constrained; the infrastructure is not.

The "more efficient than our competitors" defence

Amazon's statement that its water usage is more efficient per unit of compute than Microsoft's or Google's has a very specific resonance in British culture. It is the register of managed expectations — the language of an institution explaining that while things are not good, they are, comparatively, less bad than the available alternatives. It is what a train operating company says when it informs you that your commute is seventeen minutes longer than it was in 1995 but that its punctuality figures compare favourably with a rival franchise. It is technically accurate. It is also not reassuring. The cartoon's caption — "Amazon confirmed its data centres used 2.5 billion gallons of water last year, and noted it was more efficient than its competitors — which is, of course, exactly what you'd want to hear from your hosepipe ban" — turns this particular brand of corporate communication back on itself with considerable precision.

re:Invent in Las Vegas: the geography of the joke

AWS holds its flagship annual developer conference, re:Invent, in Las Vegas, Nevada — a desert city sustained by extensive water management infrastructure, famous for its hotel fountains, its air-conditioned casinos, and its general disposition towards consuming resources at theatrical scale. Thousands of developers travel there each year to attend sessions on cloud efficiency, sustainability, and best practices. The location is not incidental to the joke. It is, if anything, on-brand.

Ronseal and the cloud: what it says on the tin

There is a long-running British advertising slogan for a brand of wood varnish: "does exactly what it says on the tin." It has entered the language as shorthand for honest, transparent functionality — the thing that simply works as described. The cartoon inverts this. The cloud does, it transpires, do exactly what it says on the tin. The tin says "cloud." Clouds are formed by evaporated water rising into the atmosphere. The cloud infrastructure is formed, in part, by water being evaporated in the cooling process. The brand metaphor was accurate all along — it was simply that no one had followed it to its logical conclusion.

Who is Gata, and why is she sitting there?

Gata is the calico-tabby investigative mascot of the Gata Newsroom. She is rendered in full natural colour against a greyscale newsroom background — a 1970s press room of metal desks, typewriters, and strip lighting. She sits in front of the chalkboard wearing only her collar, her expression composed and forensically unimpressed. She does not point. She does not gesture. She simply sits, in the manner of an investigator who has confirmed exactly what she suspected and takes no particular pleasure in having been right. The information is on the board. She has nothing to add. The architecture diagram has always had a missing box. She found it. It drips.
