For Those Who Would Rather Read Documentation

What Is This Cartoon?

The cartoon shows Gata — the Gata Newsroom's resident investigative correspondent, a calico-tabby cat rendered in full colour against a greyscale 1970s newspaper office — standing at a large chalkboard. The board is covered in what appears to be a software engineering incident report, formatted as a git commit log and a bug-tracker ticket. The subject of the incident is not, as you might initially hope, a routine deploy gone wrong. It is the algorithmically amplified incitement of race riots in Belfast, executed on the eve of a SpaceX IPO, by a billionaire with 180 million followers and, apparently, no on-call engineer.

Gata's expression is that of a senior developer who has just been paged at eleven forty-seven on a Friday night, pulled up the deployment log, and located the problem immediately. She deeply regrets what she has found.

The Setting and the Visual Joke

The newsroom is deliberately drawn in the visual language of 1970s journalism — fluorescent strip lighting, overflowing wire-copy baskets, the grinding weight of institutional record-keeping. This is the world in which facts were gathered slowly, verified carefully, and printed with consequence. It is placed in direct contrast with the subject matter on the chalkboard: a software deployment that took milliseconds, had no reviewers, and affected twenty-five years of conflict resolution.

The selective colour technique — Gata in full colour, everything else in greyscale — is a visual signature of the Gata Newsroom. Here it also carries satirical weight: she is the only agent of scrutiny in the frame. Everyone else is too exhausted to look up.

The Chalkboard: A Git Commit Log as Incident Report

The centrepiece of the cartoon is the chalkboard, formatted to mimic the output you would see in a real software repository — specifically, a git commit pushed directly to a production environment. For non-developers, here is a translation of every line:

COMMIT: a4f7bc2 [PROD] Friday 11:47pm Author: @TechBro_Billionaire Message: "minor engagement optimisation"

A commit is a saved change to a codebase, with a unique identifier (the alphanumeric string), a timestamp, an author, and a message describing what was supposedly changed. Pushing directly to [PROD] — the live, public-facing production environment — without going through a staging or testing environment first is considered, in virtually every professional software team, to be an act of either breathtaking confidence or catastrophic negligence. The timestamp, Friday 11:47pm, is the joke within the joke: this is precisely when no sensible engineer makes irreversible changes, because there is no one available to fix them until Monday, and the weekend is long.

The commit message — "minor engagement optimisation" — is the kind of anodyne, deliberately vague description that conceals the actual nature of a change. It is the software equivalent of a planning application that describes a demolition project as "minor external alterations."

DIFF: - Good Friday Agreement (1998) [STABLE] + Good Friday Agreement (1998) [DEPRECATED]

A diff shows what has changed between the old version and the new version of a file. Lines prefixed with a minus sign are removed; lines prefixed with a plus sign are added. Here, the Good Friday Agreement — the peace deal that ended decades of violent conflict in Northern Ireland — has been changed from [STABLE] to [DEPRECATED]. In software, marking something as deprecated means it is being phased out: it still exists, but it is no longer supported and will eventually be removed. The joke is that a foundational international peace agreement has been reclassified as legacy infrastructure, quietly scheduled for end-of-life, by someone who did not write it and cannot maintain it.

CHANGED FILES: belfast_peace_process.patch ← BREAKING CHANGE ipo_quiet_period_compliance.cfg ← DELETED

A breaking change is one that is not backwards compatible — existing behaviour is destroyed, not merely modified. Flagging belfast_peace_process.patch as a breaking change is the cartoon's most precise technical joke: a patch is, in software terms, a fix applied to an existing system to correct a fault. The Good Friday Agreement was, quite literally, a patch applied to Northern Ireland after a catastrophic system failure. Marking it as a breaking change means the fix has been broken.

The deletion of ipo_quiet_period_compliance.cfg refers to the legal and regulatory restrictions placed on company executives before a public stock offering (an IPO — Initial Public Offering). During a "quiet period," executives are prohibited from making public statements that could be construed as attempting to influence the share price. Deleting this file — that is, apparently ignoring these obligations entirely — in the same commit is the cartoon noting, with extreme dryness, that basic legal compliance appears to have been an optional dependency.

REVIEWER: none TESTS RUN: none ROLLBACK PLAN: none

In any responsible software team, a change of significant consequence requires a code review — at least one other engineer must examine it, question it, and approve it before it is deployed. Tests are run to verify that the change does not break anything. A rollback plan is prepared so that if something does go wrong, the system can be returned to its previous stable state. The cartoon lists all three as absent. This is not hyperbole: algorithmically amplifying inflammatory content is not, by its nature, easily rolled back. The speech has been made. The crowd has been reached. There is no undo button for a riot.

DEPLOYMENT WINDOW: SpaceX IPO Eve AFFECTED DEPENDENCIES: 25 years of conflict resolution TICKET PRIORITY: Critical TICKET DESCRIPTION: "make it worse"

The deployment window is the scheduled time chosen for a release. Choosing the eve of a major IPO — a moment of maximum legal, financial, and reputational exposure — as the deployment window for socially destabilising content is, the cartoon suggests, either an act of supreme indifference to consequences or a demonstration that the concept of "appropriate timing" has been entirely uninstalled from the relevant decision-making process.

The ticket description, "make it worse," is the punchline. In software teams, a ticket (or issue) describes the problem to be solved or the feature to be built. It should contain context, acceptance criteria, and some indication of what success looks like. This ticket contains none of those things. It has, however, been set to Critical priority, which means it jumped the queue ahead of everything else.

The Index Card in the Corner

Attached to the chalkboard with masking tape — the universal symbol of something that does not quite belong but has been stuck there anyway — is a torn index card reading:

"Move fast and break things."
— found in a skip, California, approx. 2012

"Move fast and break things" was the internal motto of Facebook during its early growth years, later attributed broadly to Silicon Valley's startup culture. It advocated speed over caution, iteration over planning, and the acceptance of collateral damage as the price of progress. Facebook quietly retired the slogan once it became apparent that the "things" being broken included democratic institutions, mental health outcomes, and civil discourse. The motto has since become something of a cautionary tale taught in technology ethics seminars.

The detail that it was "found in a skip, California, approx. 2012" is the cartoon observing that this philosophy was not inherited or thoughtfully adopted — it was retrieved from the rubbish, dusted off, and deployed without reading the warning label attached to it, which read: "This broke Facebook. Do not reuse."

The Caption

"The pull request had 180 million approvers and no reviewers. Compliance is understood to be stress-vaping."

A pull request is a formal mechanism for proposing changes to a codebase. It is reviewed by colleagues before being merged. Having 180 million approvers refers to Elon Musk's follower count on X (formerly Twitter): the implication is that the only "review" the content received was the amplifying engagement of a mass audience, none of whom were in a position to assess the downstream consequences or reject the change. Approval by audience is not the same as approval by someone who understands the system.

The compliance team "stress-vaping" is the cartoon's acknowledgement that somewhere, in legal and regulatory departments across the financial industry, there are people whose entire professional purpose is to prevent exactly this kind of behaviour during an IPO quiet period, and that they are currently having a very bad Friday night indeed.

The Good Friday Agreement: The Context You Need

For readers outside the United Kingdom and Ireland, or younger readers for whom the 1990s are history rather than memory: Northern Ireland experienced approximately thirty years of violent ethno-nationalist and political conflict known as "the Troubles," during which paramilitary organisations on both sides conducted bombings, assassinations, and sustained campaigns of violence that claimed over three and a half thousand lives. Mainland Britain was not insulated from this — British developers of a certain age will remember bomb evacuations in city centres.

The Good Friday Agreement, signed in April 1998, was the result of years of extraordinarily difficult negotiations involving the British and Irish governments, multiple political parties in Northern Ireland, and significant engagement from the United States government. It established power-sharing arrangements, decommissioning processes, and a framework for cross-border cooperation. It is widely regarded as one of the most successful peace processes of the twentieth century.

It is not, to be direct about this, a legacy system ripe for disruption. It is a carefully maintained, hard-won arrangement whose stability depends on all parties continuing to treat it with the seriousness it deserves. The cartoon's point is that amplifying inflammatory content in Belfast without any apparent awareness of this context is the geopolitical equivalent of deleting a production database because the table names looked untidy.

Why This Is Aimed at British Software Engineers Specifically

The cartoon works on multiple registers simultaneously, but it is calibrated for an audience with a specific professional and cultural background. British software engineers will recognise:

The Friday deployment horror

The universal law that you do not push breaking changes to production on a Friday afternoon exists because weekends provide no support cover. The closer the timestamp gets to midnight, the more genuinely alarming the commit log becomes. Friday 11:47pm is not a deployment window. It is a hostage situation.

The "someone else's technical debt" archetype

Every engineering team inherits a legacy codebase with undocumented decisions, patches that hold the whole thing together, and a foundational piece of logic that nobody fully understands but everyone is afraid to touch. The correct response is not to refactor it without reading the comments. The correct response is to read the comments, especially the ones that say "DO NOT REMOVE THIS."

The vague stakeholder ticket

Stack Overflow's annual developer surveys have consistently identified "unclear requirements from stakeholders" as one of the most significant sources of developer frustration. A ticket that reads "make it worse" with no acceptance criteria, no definition of done, and Critical priority is a parody of every ticket that has ever arrived from a senior executive who knows what they want but cannot describe it in terms that allow anyone else to build it safely.

The IPO quiet period as a compliance config file

Financial regulation has a great deal in common with configuration management: it is unglamorous, it exists because bad things happened when it did not exist, and deleting it does not make the underlying risk disappear. British developers working in fintech or for publicly listed companies will be intimately familiar with the constraints of regulatory compliance and the specific anxiety of a compliance team discovering that someone has gone off-script during a sensitive period.

What Makes It Funny, and What Makes It Uncomfortable

The humour here operates through the collision of register: software engineering has a very precise, bureaucratic vocabulary for describing failure — breaking changes, deprecated dependencies, missing rollback plans — and applying that vocabulary to an act of real-world political recklessness creates a deadpan absurdity. The incident report format implies that there is a process for managing this kind of failure, when there clearly is not. The joke is that the professional language exists, the professional norms exist, and none of them were applied.

The discomfort is that the cartoon is not entirely fictional. Belfast is a real city. The Good Friday Agreement is a real document. The amplification of inflammatory content on social media platforms is a documented phenomenon with documented consequences. Gata's expression — the look of someone who has just found the commit, traced the author, and deeply regrets the entirety of the situation — is the expression of a professional who understands exactly how avoidable this was.

The IPO footnote is the cartoon's final twist of the knife: the suggestion that social destabilisation might eventually be listed as an "engagement metric" in a prospectus is not, given the past decade of platform growth reporting, entirely implausible. That is the part that makes you stop laughing.

About Gata

Gata is the Gata Newsroom's lead investigative correspondent. She is a domestic shorthair calico-tabby mix — white chest, muzzle, and paws; dark grey-black tabby stripes; orange-ginger patches; a small dark spot on the bridge of her pink nose; a simple leather collar with a brass nameplate. She appears in full colour in an otherwise greyscale world. She does not wear hats, glasses, or human clothing of any kind. She does not need to. Her bearing communicates sufficient gravity without accessories.

Her role in each cartoon is not to be surprised by what she finds. It is to have already understood it, and to be pointing at it, with the resigned precision of someone who has read the full git history and knows exactly when this started going wrong.
