On The Spot: Agile Ceremonies and the British Art of Polite, Infinite Delay

A plain-language guide to the cartoon, for anyone who has never attended a Sprint Retrospective, or who has attended too many and would like someone to confirm what they suspected.

The cartoon shows a calico cat named Gata standing beside a chalkboard in a 1970s newsroom. The board is headed Ceremonies That Could Have Been an Email and branches into four labelled boxes: Daily Standup, Sprint Retrospective, Sprint Planning, and The Email — which, the annotation explains, became a meeting. Taped to the corner is a note attributed to Dave, who suggests taking things offline every Thursday. The caption at the bottom completes the loop: the email became a call, the call became a ceremony, the ceremony generated an action to review itself, and the action remains ongoing. Dave is fine.

This is a joke about software development culture, British institutional behaviour, and a methodology called Agile. If none of those words meant anything to you, this guide will work through each layer in turn.

What Is Agile, and Where Did It Come From?

In 2001, a group of American software developers gathered in a ski resort in Utah and wrote a short document called the Agile Manifesto. It argued, in essence, that software teams should work in short bursts called sprints, talk to each other constantly, adapt quickly to change, and spend less time writing elaborate plans that would be out of date before anyone read them. Hierarchy was to be flattened. Bureaucracy was to be eliminated. The team would be self-organising and radically transparent.

The manifesto gave rise to a specific methodology called Scrum, which packaged these principles into a set of regular group meetings, referred to, with deliberate informality, as ceremonies. These included:

  • The Daily Standup: A brief, fifteen-minute meeting — conducted standing, to discourage comfort and therefore length — in which each team member says what they did yesterday, what they plan to do today, and whether anything is blocking them.
  • The Sprint Planning: A meeting at the start of each sprint in which the team decides what work to attempt in the coming fortnight.
  • The Sprint Retrospective: A meeting at the end of each sprint in which the team reflects on how they worked together and identifies improvements.

The intention was lightness. The reality, in many British organisations, was something else entirely.

The British Reception of Agile

Agile arrived in British technology companies during the 2000s and 2010s with the same energy it had in America. The difference was what happened next.

British workplace culture has a long and distinguished history of receiving imported management philosophies with apparent enthusiasm and then quietly domesticating them until they are unrecognisable. The process is never confrontational. There is no memo announcing that the new system will be modified. Instead, adjustments accumulate through a series of individually reasonable decisions, until the original purpose has been entirely replaced by a structure that resembles it only in name.

This is what the chalkboard in the cartoon is documenting. The four nodes of the org-chart — drawn to look like the formal taxonomy of a committee report — describe exactly this transformation:

DAILY STANDUP
with agenda

The Daily Standup was explicitly designed to have no agenda. You stand up, you say your three things, you leave. The addition of an agenda — particularly one with two standing items marked AOB (Any Other Business, a phrase borrowed directly from the formal minutes of committee meetings) — is a masterclass in passive structural resistance. The meeting has not been cancelled. It has been promoted.

SPRINT RETROSPECTIVE
action: review this retrospective — owner: — — due: ongoing

The Sprint Retrospective is meant to produce concrete, actionable improvements. The joke here is that the action generated by this particular retrospective is a review of the retrospective itself — with no named owner and a due date of "ongoing." This is the process eating itself. The meeting has become a meeting about whether the meeting is a good meeting, and no one has been assigned to resolve this, and it will never be resolved, and everyone involved understands this implicitly and says nothing.

SPRINT PLANNING
rescheduled from last sprint's sprint planning

Sprint Planning is the meeting in which work gets assigned. If Sprint Planning keeps being rescheduled from the previous sprint's Sprint Planning, then no sprint ever formally begins — or more precisely, sprints continue to happen but the planning meeting for them keeps being deferred into a future that never quite arrives. The work proceeds regardless. The ceremony does not.

THE EMAIL
(became a meeting)

This one is both the simplest and the most devastating. The entire point of Agile, partially, is to reduce unnecessary meetings by encouraging direct communication. The email — an asynchronous, efficient tool — has here become a meeting. The meeting, as the caption explains, then became a ceremony. The ceremony generated an action to review itself. The action is ongoing. This is the full lifecycle of British institutional drift described in a single four-word annotation.

Who Is Dave, and Why Is He Fine?

"let's take this offline" — Dave, every Thursday

Dave is a composite figure. He is every person in every Sprint Retrospective who has been raising the same concern — usually labelled something like "team communication" or "visibility of blockers" — for longer than most of his colleagues can remember. He has survived three project management tools, two restructures, and one rebranding of the team itself. His concern has not been resolved. It has been noted, actioned, re-raised, re-noted, and re-actioned. It is now effectively a permanent fixture, like a recurring calendar invite that no one has ever cancelled because no one can remember who created it.

The phrase "let's take this offline" is technically a suggestion to have a brief side conversation rather than discuss something in a group meeting. In British workplace practice, it has evolved into a polite mechanism for deferring a matter indefinitely. The item is removed from the room — which counts, socially, as progress — and enters a state of permanent undocumented suspension. Dave says this every Thursday. The matters Dave takes offline do not, in any meaningful sense, go anywhere.

The note says Dave is fine. This is important. Dave is not suffering. Dave is not frustrated. Dave has reached a state of complete equilibrium with a process that produces nothing actionable, and he participates in it with the same steady, weekly reliability as the meeting itself. Dave and the process have become one.

The Scrum Master Problem

Scrum defines a specific role called the Scrum Master. This person is not a manager. Their job is to protect the team from obstacles and to ensure the process runs as designed. In theory, a Scrum Master in a dysfunctional organisation would identify that the ceremonies had drifted from their purpose, raise this clearly, and implement corrections.

In practice, in a British organisation, the obstacle is the culture. The tendency to add agenda items to meetings, to assign actions to no one, to defer difficult conversations offline, to expand rather than streamline — these are not bugs in the system. They are features of the environment in which the system operates. You cannot file a ticket against the ambient social behaviour of an entire workforce.

So the Scrum Master, finding that the obstacles are structural and human rather than technical and individual, does what any reasonable person does in this situation: they book a room for next Thursday and invite everyone to discuss it.

The Deeper Cultural Joke: The British Civil Service Parallel

The cartoon is not only a joke about software teams. It is a joke about a pattern that repeats across British institutional life. The British Civil Service has, for centuries, demonstrated an extraordinary capacity to receive reform mandates — from politicians, from consultants, from external auditors — and to absorb them without material change. This is not achieved through obstruction. It is achieved through a kind of patient, procedural hospitality. The mandate is welcomed. A working group is formed. A report is commissioned. Recommendations are noted. The political will that produced the original mandate eventually dissipates, and the institution continues more or less as before, except that it now has a new layer of documentation describing how it is committed to change.

Enterprise Agile transformation follows this pattern with remarkable fidelity. A consultancy is engaged. A framework is introduced. The framework is adapted to local conditions. The local conditions are, specifically, the existing culture. Three years and several consultancy engagements later, the team is running something they refer to, without irony, as "how we do things here" — a bespoke hybrid that has retained the vocabulary of Agile (sprints, retrospectives, velocity, ceremonies) while discarding its operating principles (brevity, accountability, continuous improvement).

The language of disruption was adopted wholesale, placed inside existing hierarchies, and emerged the other side as a new layer of middle management with American job titles and the same decision-making authority as before, which is to say none. — Gata Newsroom editorial context

The Open Plan Office: A Footnote in the Same Story

The cartoon's newsroom setting — all heavy desks, strip lighting, and background figures who appear to be ignoring one another — is also a nod to a related import: the open plan office. This was another American concept sold on the premise of flattening hierarchy and enabling spontaneous collaboration. In British practice, it was not resisted. Instead, it was managed. Focus days were invented. Meeting rooms — glass-walled, visible, slightly performatively busy — were booked for solo work. Headphones became a universally understood social signal meaning: I am here, I am working, and if you speak to me I will respond with the minimum number of words required to make you go away.

No policy produced this. No manager mandated it. It emerged through quiet, collective, unspoken agreement — which is perhaps the most British form of institutional decision-making available.

Why the Cartoon Is Funny

The cartoon is funny for several reasons operating simultaneously.

First, it presents a genuinely absurd situation — a meeting about reviewing the meeting that reviewed the meeting, owned by no one, due forever — with the neutral, taxonomic language of a management framework. The org-chart format, the formal box labels, the annotations in the style of meeting minutes: these apply serious bureaucratic apparatus to a situation whose defining feature is the total absence of any serious outcome. The gap between the format and the content is where the joke lives.

Second, it is accurate. Anyone who has worked in a British technology team for more than a year will recognise every element. The Daily Standup with agenda items. The retrospective action that reviews the retrospective. Dave. The offline conversation that never resurfaces. These are not exaggerations. They are descriptions.

Third, no one is being blamed. The cartoon does not suggest that the individuals involved are lazy, or stupid, or malicious. Dave is fine. The ceremonies run smoothly. The committee functions. It is simply that the original purpose — to build software faster, more adaptively, with less waste — has been gently replaced by the purpose of maintaining the ceremonies themselves. And this replacement happened not through failure, but through a kind of institutional success: the British talent for receiving a foreign system and making it, over time, completely and irrevocably its own.

Cartoon Caption — Reproduced in Full "The email was sent. Dave replied-all suggesting a call. The call became a standing ceremony. The ceremony generated an action to review the ceremony. The action is ongoing. Dave is fine."

This caption is the joke in miniature. It describes a complete closed loop: a simple communication act (an email) passing through every stage of Agile ceremony inflation until it has become entirely self-referential. The final sentence — Dave is fine — is the punchline. Not because Dave's wellbeing is in doubt, but because the observation that Dave is fine implies that this state of affairs is not a crisis. It is not even a problem. It is just how things are now. Dave has adapted. The process has adapted. Everyone is fine. Nothing will change. The action is ongoing.

A Note on Gata

Gata is the Newsroom's regular correspondent and analyst. She appears in the cartoon in full colour against a greyscale background, standing beside the chalkboard with one paw resting on its lower edge and an expression that conveys, without words, the following: I have been watching this for some time. I have formed a view. The view is not optimistic, but it is accurate, and I have laid it out here for your inspection.

She is not wearing a hat, or glasses, or any item of clothing. She does not need to. The chalkboard speaks for itself, and Gata has learned that the most effective thing one can do in a room full of people generating actions owned by no one is to present the evidence clearly, hold the viewer's gaze, and let the silence do the rest.

Dave, presumably, will suggest taking it offline.
