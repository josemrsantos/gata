ON THE SPOT: How Britain Quietly Defeated Agile by Becoming It

A cultural briefing for operators on the satirical cartoon published under the title ON THE SPOT, featuring Gata at a chalkboard taxonomy of ceremonies that could have been an email — and Dave, who is fine.

Gata Newsroom Intelligence Desk  ·  Explainer Edition  ·  Target audience: British software engineers and developers

What the Cartoon Shows

Gata — the newsroom's resident investigative calico — stands beside a large chalkboard in a greyscale 1970s newspaper office, rendered in full colour against the monochrome background. One forepaw rests on the lower edge of the board. Her expression is flat, analytical, and faintly exhausted: the face of someone who has attended this meeting before and fully expects to attend it again.

The chalkboard bears the heading ON THE SPOT and presents an org-chart taxonomy rooted in a single box: CEREMONIES THAT COULD HAVE BEEN AN EMAIL. Four branches descend from it.

CEREMONIES THAT COULD HAVE BEEN AN EMAIL ├─ DAILY STANDUP — with agenda ├─ SPRINT RETROSPECTIVE — action: review this retrospective — owner: — — due: ongoing ├─ SPRINT PLANNING — rescheduled from last sprint's sprint planning └─ THE EMAIL — (became a meeting)

"let's take this offline" — Dave, every Thursday  [taped to lower-right corner]

Panel Caption (printed below the cartoon)

"The email was sent. Dave replied-all suggesting a call. The call became a standing ceremony. The ceremony generated an action to review the ceremony. The action is ongoing. Dave is fine."

The 1970s newsroom setting is deliberate. It positions the critique outside the present moment — gesturing at a long institutional continuity — while the colour treatment on Gata signals that she alone is paying full attention. Everyone else has been here before.

News and Industry Background: The Agile Transformation

Agile software development, and specifically the Scrum framework, emerged from the United States in the late 1990s and early 2000s. The Agile Manifesto (2001) declared, with some missionary confidence, that software teams should value individuals over processes, working software over documentation, and responding to change over following a plan. It was explicitly anti-bureaucratic.

Scrum introduced a specific vocabulary: the Sprint (a fixed period, usually two weeks, of focused development work), the Daily Standup (a brief daily check-in of fifteen minutes, no chairs, no agenda — by design), the Sprint Planning meeting, and the Sprint Retrospective (a structured reflection on how the team is working). The role of Scrum Master was created to remove obstacles from the team's path.

From roughly 2005 to the present, Agile and Scrum swept through British enterprises, consultancies, banks, media companies, and public-sector technology programmes. Enormous sums were spent on certifications, coaching engagements, and transformation programmes. The vocabulary landed. The ceremonies were adopted. And then — in offices across the country — something happened to them.

The Core Observation

Agile was not resisted. It was absorbed. The ceremonies survived and multiplied. The spirit — radical transparency, rapid iteration, the abolition of unnecessary process — did not always survive with them. What emerged, in many organisations, was Scrum-shaped bureaucracy: all the meetings, with their own agendas, their own sub-meetings, and their own actions to review the meetings.

This is the situation the cartoon addresses. It is not a niche complaint. Every British software engineer who has worked in a mid-to-large organisation will recognise the taxonomy on the chalkboard immediately and probably viscerally. The joke lands because it is, in many respects, a documentary.

Cultural References Decoded

Ceremony 1

The Daily Standup "with agenda"

The Daily Standup was explicitly designed to have no agenda. It has three questions: what did you do yesterday, what will you do today, is anything blocking you. Fifteen minutes. The joke in the annotation "with agenda" is that the anti-meeting has become a meeting. In practice, many British tech teams have added agenda items, introductory rounds, announcements, and — in a number of documented cases — a standing agenda item to review the format of the standup. The standup now requires its own standup.

Ceremony 2

The Sprint Retrospective — "action: review this retrospective — owner: — — due: ongoing"

The Retrospective is designed to generate concrete, owned, time-bound actions that improve how the team works. The annotation on the board captures its most common failure mode with surgical precision: the action produced by the retrospective is an action to review the retrospective, owned by no one, and marked "ongoing" — a status that, in British project management culture, is understood to mean "immortal." The retrospective has become its own subject matter and its own obstacle, simultaneously.

The "owner: — —" field deserves particular attention. In any collaborative tool (Jira, Trello, Linear, a shared spreadsheet — all of which are referenced obliquely in the cartoon's cultural brief), an unassigned action is an action that will not be taken. The double dash is a small, devastating detail.

Ceremony 3

Sprint Planning — "rescheduled from last sprint's sprint planning"

Sprint Planning is the ceremony that must happen before work begins. Rescheduling it from the previous sprint's planning session means work either started without it, or did not start at all. The annotation captures a specific and widely experienced failure: the planning meeting for a time-boxed iteration was itself not completed within the time box. The sprint to plan the sprint ran over. There is no further comment. None is needed.

Ceremony 4

The Email — "(became a meeting)"

This is the structural joke of the entire taxonomy. The tree begins with ceremonies that could have been an email. The fourth node is the email. The email, having been sent, generated a reply-all suggesting a call, which became a recurring ceremony. The very unit that could have replaced the meeting has itself become a meeting and joined the taxonomy. The system is closed. It is self-referential. It is, in its way, perfect.

The caption at the bottom of the panel narrates this process explicitly, in the dry, declarative tone of a parliamentary committee interim report.

The Dave Note

"let's take this offline" — Dave, every Thursday

The scrap of paper taped to the chalkboard is arguably the cartoon's richest detail. Dave is not named in any ceremony. Dave is not a Scrum Master, a product owner, or a sprint stakeholder. Dave is the person who has been raising the same concern — team communication — since before the company cycled through Trello, Jira, Linear, and back to a shared spreadsheet. Dave is institutional memory, crystallised into a single recurring phrase.

"Let's take this offline" is American corporate English meaning "let's discuss this separately." In the British context the cartoon is addressing, it has acquired a secondary meaning: the matter will now enter a permanent, undocumented holding pattern. Nothing taken offline in a British retrospective has ever returned with a resolution. Dave knows this. Dave says it anyway. Dave is fine.

"Dave is fine" in the panel caption is the cartoon's emotional core. Dave is not a victim. Dave is not frustrated. Dave is the process, and the process continues, and Dave continues with it, every Thursday, without complaint or apparent distress. This is not resignation. It is something closer to a professional vocation.

Why These References Resonate with British Software Engineers

British tech workers occupy a specific cultural position. They are employed in an industry whose dominant intellectual frameworks — Agile, Lean, OKRs, psychological safety workshops, radical candour — are almost entirely American in origin and were exported with a degree of evangelical confidence. The reception in Britain has been, characteristically, neither rejection nor full adoption, but a quiet, thorough domestication.

The open-plan office did not disappear in the face of British opposition. It was simply navigated around: focus days were invented unilaterally, glass-walled meeting rooms were booked for solitary work, and a shared, unspoken convention developed in which headphones mean do not attempt eye contact under any circumstances. The office remains open-plan. The culture within it became something different.

The MBA wave of the 1980s provides the template. Disruption-era management language was adopted wholesale — the titles, the frameworks, the vocabulary of transformation — and placed inside existing hierarchies, where it emerged as a new layer of middle management with American job titles and, in practice, no additional decision-making authority. The language changed. The structure did not.

The British Civil Service tradition of receiving reform mandates and filing them under "noted" until the political will dissolves is the deepest cultural root. The enterprise Agile transformation that, three years and four consultancy engagements later, has produced a bespoke version of Scrum that its practitioners describe, without irony, as "how we do things here" is its direct commercial heir. The reform was real. Its output became the new tradition.

Why the Tone Works

British satirical wit, particularly in professional contexts, operates through understatement, the precise deployment of bureaucratic language against itself, and a refusal to editorialise. The cartoon's caption does not say the situation is absurd. It describes the situation in the neutral register of meeting minutes, and allows the reader to arrive at absurdity unassisted. Gata's expression — flat, analytical, faintly tired — performs the same function. She is not outraged. She is informed. This is worse.

What Makes the Satirical Angle Effective

The cartoon works on three levels simultaneously, each reinforcing the others.

The structural level. The taxonomy tree is itself a piece of bureaucratic documentation — organised, hierarchical, formally presented. It uses the visual language of the very thing it is satirising. A framework designed to critique process-for-its-own-sake is presented as a process chart. The medium is the message, and the message is that there is no exit from the form.

The detail level. Each annotation on the tree is a small, complete joke that will be immediately legible to anyone who has worked in an Agile environment. "Owner: — —" is not an invented absurdity; it is a specific field in a specific type of tool, left empty, and the emptiness is the point. "Rescheduled from last sprint's sprint planning" requires no explanation to the target audience because the target audience has rescheduled this meeting. The annotations are not exaggerations. They are observations.

The emotional level. Dave. The panel caption ends with "Dave is fine." It does not say that Dave is happy, or that Dave is correct, or that Dave's concern about team communication has been addressed. It says Dave is fine. Dave has achieved a state of serene continuity within a system that cannot act on his concern and cannot stop generating the forum in which he raises it. Dave has found his equilibrium. This is the cartoon's deepest joke, and it is not entirely unkind.

For British software engineers — who have, in aggregate, spent thousands of hours in standup meetings with agendas, retrospectives with no actionable outcomes, and planning sessions for planning sessions — the cartoon offers something more valuable than critique. It offers recognition. The chalkboard is a mirror. Gata is looking at them. Her expression says: yes, I know. I have been here every Thursday too.

Operator Notes: Contextual Sensitivity

The cartoon is satirical but not hostile. It does not blame individuals, teams, or organisations. Dave is explicitly described as fine, and as a creature of the process rather than a victim of it. The Scrum Master is portrayed with sympathy — a role designed to remove obstacles that turns out to be embedded in a culture that is the obstacle. The target is systemic: it is the gap between a methodology's intent and the cultural context into which it is received.

The target audience — British software engineers and developers — will recognise themselves in this cartoon and are highly likely to share it, particularly internally. The humour is inclusive: it assumes the audience is intelligent, experienced, and in on the joke. It does not condescend. It does not prescribe a solution. It presents the situation with the forensic neutrality of a chalkboard taxonomy, and leaves the rest to the reader.

Operators distributing this content in enterprise or B2B contexts should note that the specific ceremonies named — Daily Standup, Sprint Retrospective, Sprint Planning — are standard Scrum terminology and will be immediately recognised by the primary audience without explanation. The cartoon's effectiveness depends on this instant recognition. No glossary is required. Dave requires no introduction. Dave is already in the room.
