<verdict>
CULTURAL ANGLE: Amazon has announced, with what can only be described as commendable brass neck, that its data centres consumed 2.5 billion gallons of water last year — and would like you to know that this is actually *impressive*, because Microsoft and Google are apparently even worse. For British software engineers who have spent the last decade being told to turn off the tap while brushing their teeth, and who watched their local councils introduce hosepipe bans during a drizzly August, there is something almost magnificent about the scale of this particular spin. The industry that insists on calling its storage infrastructure "data lakes" has, it turns out, been filling them quite literally. AWS, the very platform your microservices are probably running on right now, is essentially the world's most expensive, least visible water feature — and it wants a gold star for only using *this* much. It is, in the grand tradition of British corporate life, the equivalent of a manager explaining that while the project is six months late and twice over budget, it is nonetheless ahead of where Dave's team left off.

REFERENCES:
- During the 2022 UK drought, millions of households faced hosepipe bans and were urged to take shorter showers; the contrast with a single company's nine-billion-odd litres of annual water consumption requires no further embellishment.
- "Data lakes" is a genuine enterprise architecture term, used earnestly in planning meetings by the same industry now filling them with cooling water — the metaphor has become uncomfortably literal.
- British developers will recognise the OKR-speak rhythm of "we're more efficient than our competitors" as the same energy behind sprint retrospectives where the team celebrates missing targets by slightly less than last quarter.
- Amazon's own warehouse working conditions — documented extensively in the UK press, including workers reportedly avoiding toilet breaks — make the revelation that the buildings *definitely* have enough water for the servers land with a particular dryness.
- "Waterfall methodology" is the development approach every British dev team claims to have abandoned in favour of Agile; AWS appears to have quietly kept it as a cooling strategy.
- Reporting the figure in *gallons* rather than litres is the kind of move that will irritate a British engineer before they've even processed the scale — like receiving API documentation in imperial units.
