Explainer: Amazon's Data Centre Water Consumption, and Why British Developers Are Not Impressed

The News in Brief

Amazon disclosed in its 2024 sustainability reporting that its global data centres consumed approximately 2.5 billion gallons of water during the previous year. Rather than presenting this figure as a cause for alarm, the company elected to frame it as a marker of relative efficiency — pointing out, with considerable confidence, that rivals Microsoft, Google, and Meta use water even less efficiently. Amazon, in other words, announced a number large enough to fill several reservoirs and then waited for applause.

The figure equates to roughly nine billion litres, which is the sort of quantity that tends to concentrate the mind — unless, apparently, you are writing an infrastructure sustainability report, in which case it is a Q4 win worth carrying into the next sprint.

Why This Is Funny, and to Whom

The cartoon is aimed squarely at British software engineers and developers: a professionally sceptical audience with a high tolerance for technical absurdity and a low tolerance for corporate euphemism. These are people who attend sprint retrospectives where the team celebrates missing targets by slightly less than last quarter, who have absorbed the vocabulary of Agile methodology without necessarily believing any of it, and who have almost certainly attended a planning session in which someone used the phrase "data lake" in complete sincerity. The joke works on several levels simultaneously, which is the respectable minimum for dry British wit.

Cultural Reference: The 2022 Hosepipe Bans

During the summer of 2022, the United Kingdom experienced its driest spell in decades. Several water companies imposed hosepipe bans — legal restrictions preventing households from watering their gardens or washing their cars — and public messaging urged people to take shorter showers, not leave the tap running while brushing their teeth, and generally behave as though water were a finite resource. This occurred during what was, by most metrics, a fairly recognisable British summer, meaning it also rained quite a bit. The cognitive dissonance was not lost on the population.

The contrast between a campaign asking individual households to save a few litres per day and a single company consuming nine billion litres annually for server cooling is the kind of arithmetic that does not require a calculator to feel wrong. For a British developer who dutifully turned off the tap in 2022, the Amazon figure lands not with outrage but with a very particular, very tired recognition.

Cultural Reference: "Data Lakes" — The Metaphor That Got Away

"Data lake" is a genuine and widely used enterprise architecture term. It refers to a centralised repository — typically cloud-based — where large volumes of raw data are stored in their native format until needed. The term is used earnestly in technical planning documents, architecture diagrams, and Confluence pages across every sector of British industry. It is also, it turns out, an accidentally accurate description of what is happening to the cooling water at the physical facilities underpinning those very same cloud platforms. The industry coined a liquid metaphor and then fulfilled it literally. The cartoon's third panel, showing a funnel labelled "YOUR MICROSERVICES" with water cascading into a pool labelled "DATA LAKE (LITERAL)," is not really a joke so much as a diagram.

Cultural Reference: Waterfall Methodology

In software development, "waterfall" is the traditional, sequential approach to building systems: requirements first, then design, then implementation, then testing, in a single cascading flow. British development teams spent much of the 2010s being told to abandon waterfall in favour of Agile — iterative, flexible, sprint-based delivery. Most teams made the transition, or at least claimed to. The phrase "we're Agile now" became a staple of onboarding decks. The cartoon's suggestion that AWS has retained waterfall methodology — just not in the software sense — is the sort of pun that a British developer will groan at and then immediately repeat to a colleague.

Cultural Reference: OKR-Speak and Sprint Velocity

OKRs — Objectives and Key Results — are a goal-setting framework used extensively in the technology industry. Sprint velocity is a measure of how much work a development team completes per iteration. Both terms have a particular rhetorical quality: they are capable of reframing failure as nuanced progress. A team that shipped less than planned can note that velocity is "trending in the right direction." A company that used billions of gallons of water can report an "efficiency improvement" relative to competitors. The second panel of the cartoon reproduces this logic faithfully — "Q4 WIN: USED SLIGHTLY LESS WATER THAN THE OTHERS" — and it is funny precisely because every British developer has sat in the meeting where this sentence was delivered without irony.

Cultural Reference: Amazon Warehouse Conditions

Amazon's UK warehouse operations have been the subject of extensive reporting regarding worker welfare, including accounts of staff reportedly reluctant to take toilet breaks due to productivity pressure. This context lends a particular edge to the revelation that Amazon's data centres have, demonstrably, no shortage of water — the buildings are consuming it in quantities that dwarf most municipal supplies. The servers are well-hydrated. The cartoon does not state this directly; it does not need to. The British audience will arrive there without assistance.

Cultural Reference: Reporting in Gallons

The United Kingdom officially adopted the metric system for trade and commerce decades ago. British engineers work in litres, metres, and kilograms. Gallons persist only in certain contexts — draught beer, for instance, and the fuel efficiency of older vehicles — but they are not the unit a technically literate British reader expects to encounter in an infrastructure sustainability report published in 2025 or 2026. Reporting 2.5 billion gallons rather than approximately 9.5 billion litres is the kind of presentation choice that will irritate a British software engineer before they have even begun to process the underlying figure. It reads as an attempt to deploy an unfamiliar unit in order to make a large number feel more abstract. The chalkboard annotation in the cartoon's first panel — "that's litres if you live in a country that has moved on" — captures this irritation with the precision of a well-placed comment in a code review.

What Makes the Satirical Angle Work

The cartoon's effectiveness rests on the gap between the language Amazon uses and the reality it describes. The language is corporate and optimistic: efficiency metrics, competitive benchmarking, implicit calls for recognition. The reality is nine billion litres of water consumed annually by cooling infrastructure, reported to a country that was asked to stop watering its begonias. Gata — the cartoon's presenter, a calico-tabby cat whose expression across all three panels moves from tired scepticism to resigned comprehension — embodies the audience's response: not shock, not fury, but the particular British acceptance of someone who suspected this was the case all along and finds no comfort in being right.

The three-panel structure follows the classic setup-escalation-punchline rhythm, but the punchline is not a twist — it is a convergence. By the third panel, the water metaphors (data lakes, waterfall, hosepipe bans, cooling systems) have collapsed into a single image, and the joke is that none of this was ever metaphorical at all. The industry built its abstractions on top of a very physical resource, gave those abstractions watery names, and is now surprised that someone has noticed the plumbing.

For a British software engineer, the final caption — "waterfall methodology has, in fact, been fully retained. Just not in software development" — works because it is technically accurate, professionally cutting, and entirely unsurprising. Which is, more or less, the definition of dry wit.
