What the Cartoon Is About: Meta Announces That You Can Move Your Photos Around Now, and Someone Apparently Had to Tell the Press

In June 2025, Instagram — the photo-sharing platform owned by Meta, a company valued at approximately one trillion dollars — issued a formal press release to announce a new feature: users may now reorder the photographs displayed on their profile grid. They can move photo number four to position number one. They can shuffle things about. Freely, the press release said. Freely.

The cartoon is about how funny that is, if you write software for a living.

Why is it funny to software developers?

To understand the joke, you need to know one thing about how this feature works under the surface. A profile grid is, in computing terms, an ordered list — a sequence of items stored in a database in a particular arrangement. Allowing a user to reorder that list requires changing the numbers that record each item's position. In the programming language most commonly used for this kind of task, the operation looks something like this:

array.splice(fromIndex, 1) — remove the item from its current position.
array.splice(toIndex, 0, item) — insert it at its new position.

That is, roughly speaking, the entire feature. Two lines of logic, some surrounding plumbing, and a database column to store the order. An experienced developer, given a quiet Tuesday and a reasonable set of existing infrastructure, could produce a working version in a day and a half. The chalkboard in the cartoon estimates one and a half days, and this is not being ungenerous.

Meta, meanwhile, has been operating Instagram since 2012. The feature did not exist until 2025. Someone at The Verge wrote an article about it. Meta's communications team used the word "freely" in a way that implies the previous absence of this capability was, in some sense, a constraint worth remarking upon having lifted.

For a developer, this is a specific kind of comic wrongness — not outrageous, not enraging, but quietly, almost philosophically absurd. Like watching a very large, very expensive organisation book a meeting to announce that they have found the biscuit tin.

The JIRA ticket on the chalkboard

JIRA is a project-management tool used by most software teams to track work. Tasks are recorded as "tickets" — entries in a system, each with a priority level, a status, and a creation date. The cartoon shows a fictional ticket: number 4471, titled "Allow Photo Reorder," marked as Priority 2 ("Nice to Have"), sitting in the backlog, opened in March 2019.

This is a joke that every British developer will recognise instantly. In most software organisations, there exists a class of ticket — obviously useful, technically trivial, nobody's urgent priority — that sits undisturbed in the backlog for years. It is marked P2, which means "we acknowledge this should exist." It is reviewed at planning meetings and politely deferred. Then, one day, a product manager discovers it, reclassifies it as a "Q3 strategic initiative," and it ships with a press release. The ticket is probably still open somewhere, marked "Done," but the original estimate was never updated and the story points were never closed properly. Every developer reading this knows exactly which ticket at their own company this describes.

The British cultural ideal of the silent deploy

There is an unspoken professional code among British developers that good work ships quietly. You push the fix. You close the ticket. You say nothing. If it is working correctly, nobody will notice anything changed — and that is the point. The highest compliment a change can receive is that users simply find things working better one day, with no fanfare, no announcement, and certainly no press release.

The absolute antithesis of this ideal is what Meta has done: commissioned communications, drafted copy, and invited journalists to cover two lines of array manipulation as though it were a product milestone. For a British developer raised on the quiet dignity of the three-line Friday afternoon hotfix — pushed to production, deployed, done, say nothing, go home — this is not so much offensive as it is deeply meditative in its wrongness. You do not call a press conference about array.splice(). You simply do not.

The <details> element's sympathy card

At the bottom of the chalkboard in the cartoon, a small chalk note reads: "The <details> element filed a sympathy card. It took eleven years."

This requires a brief history lesson. The <details> element is a feature of standard HTML — the language used to build web pages — that creates a collapsible section of content: click a heading, the content expands; click again, it collapses. This is an extremely common and useful interface pattern. Developers had been building it manually, using JavaScript workarounds, for decades. The <details> element was first formally proposed in 2009. It did not achieve consistent, reliable support across all major browsers until around 2020 — eleven years later.

The joke is that the <details> element — the patron saint of obviously-useful features that took an unconscionably long time to arrive — is expressing solidarity with Instagram's photo reorder function. They are kindred spirits. Both features are simple. Both were clearly needed. Both took far longer than any reasonable person would predict. The <details> element, having waited eleven years to be universally supported, recognises a fellow traveller.

Barclays, Lloyds, and dark mode

The cartoon's cultural context also nods to a well-worn grievance among British developers: the banking app dark mode saga. Dark mode — which displays white text on a dark background rather than dark text on a white one — is, technically, achieved by writing a short piece of CSS code that detects a user's system preference and swaps a colour palette. It is, in terms of implementation effort, not a large task. Barclays and Lloyds, two major British banks, took years to ship it, despite it being among the most-requested features from users. Developers who consider dark mode basic screen hygiene — not a luxury, not a feature, but a reasonable default that has existed in operating systems for years — watched this process with the resigned patience of people who have stopped expecting things to be straightforward.

The Instagram announcement lands in this same tradition: a feature whose absence was not justified by technical complexity, whose arrival is accompanied by communication that implies achievement, and whose actual implementation would be unremarkable in any well-functioning engineering team's sprint.

The GDS standard

The British government's Government Digital Service operates on a useful heuristic: if a feature cannot be explained to a non-technical minister in one sentence, it may be overengineered. "Users can move their photos around" passes this test with considerable room to spare. GDS, working at the pace of a reasonably motivated public-sector team, would have shipped this in a fortnight, announced it in a changelog, and moved on. Meta, working at the pace of a trillion-dollar organisation with quarterly OKR cycles and stakeholder alignment workshops, shipped it in 2025 and sent it to The Verge.

Who is Gata, and what is her expression?

Gata is the investigative mascot of Gata Newsroom — a calico-tabby cat who appears in the publication's satirical cartoons. She is depicted in full colour against a greyscale newsroom setting, standing before a chalkboard in the manner of a journalist reviewing evidence. She wears a dark leather collar with a brass nameplate. She wears no other clothing and carries no accessories, because she is a cat, and also because she has standards.

Her expression in this cartoon is described as composed, slightly tired, and highly intelligent — specifically, the face of someone who has read the press release twice and found it, on balance, worse the second time. This is the correct response to the press release in question.

The cartoon's printed caption reads: "Meta confirmed Tuesday that users may now freely reorder photographs on their profile grid. The engineering team was unavailable for comment, as there was no reason for them to be available for comment."

The caption is the final turn of the joke. The engineers who built this feature had nothing to say to the press — not because they were busy, not because they were unavailable, but because there was nothing to say. They moved items in an array. They changed some index values. They pushed the change. The press release was somebody else's department entirely, and the engineers, one assumes, had already moved on to the next ticket.

In summary

The cartoon is laughing — quietly, drily, and with considerable precision — at the gap between the complexity of a thing and the ceremony surrounding it. Instagram's photo reorder feature is, technically, a small and sensible improvement. Nobody disputes that users might want it. The joke is entirely about the production: the press release, the word "freely," the implied sense of accomplishment, and the extraordinary institutional machinery that a trillion-dollar company appears to require in order to ship what any competent developer, left alone with a laptop and a reasonable sprint, would have done before lunch on Wednesday and mentioned to no one.

The JIRA ticket is still open somewhere. It probably has a comment from 2021 asking whether this is still relevant. Nobody replied.
